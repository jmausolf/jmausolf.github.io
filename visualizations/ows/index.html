UNDERSTANDING OCCUPY'S IMPACT

In the analysis of social movements, emphasis typically falls upon legislative gains, but movements can also have hard to foresee effects. One possible impact is our conception and awareness of inequality, and one prominent outlet to observe this change is the federal government, namely the United States Congress.

Although Occupy did not achieve much legislatively, what impacts did the movement have on changing the dialogue of inequality in the United States Congress?

METHODS

Data for this project was collected between 2009-2016 from the Government Printing Office, which chronicles the United States Congressional Record. This website has rate limiters that prevent rapid download of data. To work around this issue, a time delay was built in to the Python script. To augment faster and more efficient download, the full PDF was downloaded using a Python based wget procedure, which were converted to text subsequently using a bash script.

A subsequent Python and NLTK script was run on the data to assemble a dataset of keyword and phrase counts. Over two hundred keywords and phrases were counted. Two primary categories, "inequality" and "fair share" stand out. This data was merged with datasets on Occupy Wall Street arrests, Google search trends data, national economic data, and data on media coverage.

The observed keyword and phrase counts were examined graphically in relation to Occupy Wall Street arrests and the data were analyzed further using time series analysis to establish if Occupy arrests predict the increased discussion of inequality, all else equal.

Congressional Discussion of Inequality

in Response to Occupy Wall Street



The observed relationship between Occupy arrests and congressional rhetoric is statistically significant. Using ARFIMA models to conduct time series analysis, current day arrests and arrests one day ago predict increased Congressional discussion of inequality keywords and fair share keywords. This is true while controlling for economic conditions (S&P500 and unemployment rate), public perception of income inequality and Occupy Wall Street (via Google trends), front page and online news coverage of Occupy, and the number of words in total for each date of the Congressional record, 2009-2015.
